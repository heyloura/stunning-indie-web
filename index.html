Solo-ing Shape Up By 37Signals

Loura

I remember reading Shape Up when it came out and loving it. I forwarded it to my manager, who I’m pretty sure never read it. But at that time I was just supporting the old rickety web application and putting in the occasional small new feature (despite me practically begging to fix performance issues). Now that the rickety old application is on it’s last legs and about to crash and burn, I need to rebuild it. So I’m going to reread Shape Up, take notes, and see if I can find someway to make it work solo.

Do It Yourself.

They assume a team of one designer and one/two programmers. I’m just little old me with a manager who can help with domain knowledge. 

Since building and shaping are two separate tracks and I am only one person … I probably shouldn’t do both at once. So I need to figure out if it is more effective to shape all the essential pieces of the project first. Or follow an alternating pattern. 

Luckily since I’m rebuilding, I already know what is essential in the application. As I told my boss earlier this month, “If out app crashes and I can’t get it back up, what’s the minimum thing I can create that will keep our non-profit in business?” 

Why implement the ideas in Shape Up? 

Because projects go on and on without an end in sight. 

  • Product managers can’t think strategically 
  • Defined by: six week cycles (max), shaped work, full responsibility 
  • This book is meant to help with the risk of not shipping on time 
  • Projects should not have rabbit holes or tangled inter-dependencies. 
  • Projects should be one meaningful piece - end to end. 
  • Capped @ six weeks

Shaped Work 

Has the following properties: 

  • It’s rough so that the work can be estimated and the developers and designers can use their own judgment. Try and fall somewhere between a wireframe and words. 
  • It’s solved so that main elements are there. I.E. there is clear direction 
  • It’s bounded we know what not to do and it has a fixed amount of time 

 It’s key to ask the right questions - what are we solving? Why does it matter? etc… 

 It's Not Development Work 

  • A team builds what has already been shaped. 
  • Shaper’s shape new projects which may or may not be built 

There Are Four Steps 

At the end of each step you can always put the work down and shape something else or re-vist an earlier step if the boundaries are being violated. 

  1.  🔏Create proper boundaries. Which must contain three main components; a raw idea, an appetite, and a narrow problem definition.
  2. 🔏Rough out the estimate with the right level of detail. This is not a spec, more like the rules of the game. 
  3. 🔏 Find risks and patch rabbit holes - tackle the right problems in the right order. 
  4. 👥 Bring it together - write the pitch so people with less context can understand and evaluate it. 

 Emoji Key: 🔏 Private work … 👥 Public to team

It was my freshmen year at University in 2003 and I remember my first Computer Science class. I was super intimidated and the room being filled with almost all men didn't help. It remained that way throughout my years of school. I often wondered, when I entered into a new class, where the other women were. Computer Science has such a rich history with women that I only learned about years after I left school. Why aren't these stories acknowledged more? Why aren't they shared in middle school and in high school? Or even in college level classes? 

Pictures and stories like these would have helped when I was younger.
